Organic Hair Care

A surprising amount of my childhood was spent on watching my female caregivers look after themselves and their hair. While that probably sounds selfish...it was more a matter of being included in some amount of their personal grooming. I suppose that's because I was born at the start of the 70's...just as Vidal Sassoon slapped his name on a wide range of multinational hair care products and convinced everyone to change the way they groomed their manes.

My formative years were spent watching the older women in my family tend their hair and skin with basic products they made at home or bought inexpensively. The younger ones were lathering and slathering a collection of "modern" chemicals through their locks and moaning about the results. Too dry, too frizzy, too lank. Shampoo, conditioner, hair spray, gel, mousse, pomade...if it could be packaged in a colorful container in the Health and Beauty aisle, someone in my family was using it.

The grown-ups always looked impeccable with their "old fashioned" soap and lotions. Thinking about it now I suppose it was clever marketing combined with the normal youthful disdain for the choices made by adults that steered us into ignoring the lessons of matriarchs.

As an adult firmly settled into my own middle age, their choices seem much more reasonable.

Cocoa Dry Shampoo

Whether you want to limit how often you wash your hair or you're just a bit shiny and on the way out the door, a dry shampoo is useful to have on hand.

For my dark hair I use equal parts unsweetened cocoa powder and arrowroot power. Both can be purchased in the baking aisle of your local market. I usually mix it up in 1/2 cup batches (1/4 cup cocoa, 1/4 cup arrowroot). Blondes can use plain arrowroot, and gingery redheads can use the cocoa version cut with cinnamon.

Dust it through your roots (a sugar shaker or fluffy powder brush can be recycled to apply it) and then brush your hair briskly.

Voila! Soft, manageable, controllable hair without the grease! As an added bonus, sometimes there's the SLIGHTEST hint of chocolate in the air.

Hair Cleaning

I remember being very young and watching my Sicilian great grandmother comb out her incredibly long silver and black hair. She'd brush it thoroughly for what seemed like an hour (but was probably closer to 10 or 15 minutes), then put it up into two braids woven into a sort of coronet. She would sometimes wash it in front of me at her sink. She'd lather her hands with a bar of castile soap and only soap her scalp. Rinsing the soap out of her hair would clean the strands. When I was a little older and would get to stay with her for longer visits during the winter, I'd sometimes get to watch her coat her fingertips with her "good" olive oil and rub it into her scalp before washing. She only washed her hair once a week, but I don't remember any sense of her being smelly or dirty, quite the opposite. She always looked impeccable and smelled like a flowery talcum powder.

For years I only cleaned my hair with a dab of liquid castile soap. I can't get it easily now, so I've switched to using baking soda and apple cider vinegar to clean my hair. Unlike other "no shampoo" people who dab on a little natural oil after cleaning, I use mine before the wash. I find it helps keep my scalp from getting either too oily or too dry and itchy, and there's no greasy feeling after.

I put a little olive oil or grapeseed oil on my fingertips and massage it into my scalp when I'm oiling my face. In the shower, I pump some baking soda dissolved in water into my hand and massage that through my hair before rinsing. I then seal my hair using either diluted lemon juice or diluted apple cider vinegar, and rinse it a final time with water.

As mentioned on the skin care page, I also finger-comb my lemon and honey mask through my hair. It ends up extra clean and soft after doing so and brightens my auburn highlights.

Measurements: Dilute one tablespoon baking soda per 8 oz water. Dilute one tablespoon apple cider vinegar per 8 oz water. Put in separate pump or squeeze bottles.

Flaxseed Oil Setting Lotion

I'll confess I haven't (yet) worked up the courage to try this one. I remember my Polish grandmother going to the salon to get her hair teased and set for special occasions. For her regular weekly routine she boiled seeds in water and then combed the "slime" through her hair. I remember it somehow being both soft and immobile.

I think she may have made a setting lotion from flax seeds. I cooked it up once but it was so gross looking I couldn't bear to put it in my hair. Check back and one of these days I may have finally tried it.

This seems to be a common version of the recipe (culled from multiple internet sources).

Flax Seed Hair Setting Lotion

  • 3/4 cup water
  • 1 tablespoon flax seeds

Combine in a pot and bring to a boil. Reduce the heat and simmer for 10-15 minutes. Strain the seeds before it cools and gels.

Comb through your hair and set normally.
