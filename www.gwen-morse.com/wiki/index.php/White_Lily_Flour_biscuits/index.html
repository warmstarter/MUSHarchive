"White Lily Flour" Biscuits

The original measurements from the White Lily Flour bag (and their commercial cookbook)

Ingredients

  • 2 cups biscuit flour or all purpose flour (may adjust for texture, use no more than 3 cups)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1/4 cup butter, Crisco vegetable shortening, or margarine
  • 3/4 buttermilk (more or less to achieve a sticky dough)

The secret to good biscuits is keeping the ingredients cold. The leavener (baking powder) starts working as soon as it comes in contact with any liquid, so move fast and do as little mixing as possible.

Directions for Tender biscuits: To make tender biscuits, you need to avoid overworking the dough. Tender biscuits should fluffy inside when you break them open and make fine crumbs.

Add 2 cups flour, salt, and baking powder into a large mixing bowl. Stir to mix. Dice the butter into small squares and make sure they're chilled to be very firm. Add butter and cut into the flour until you get dime-sized chunks of the flour/butter mixture. If you like you can pulse these ingredients in a food processor 3-4 times. The spinning motion from using the food processor grater will also coat the butter shavings with flour. Alternatively, you can freeze the butter and grate it into the flour mixture. What you want is very fine dots or shavings of butter that you can barely see.

If you used a food processor, move ingredients to a mixing bowl. If the dry ingredients are mixed with the buttermilk in a food processor it will over mix the dough and create tough biscuits. Make a well in the center of the dry ingredients, and pour in the buttermilk, a little at a time. Mix gently with a fork until you get a sticky dough texture. DO NOT KNEAD.

Turn dough onto a floured surface. Dust top with small amount of flour, press out to 1-inch thickness with your fingertips. DO NOT ROLL.

Directions for Flaky biscuits: Flaky biscuits are made up of many fine layers. When you break them open, they should break apart into leaves. They tend to be worked a bit more than tender biscuits and tend to be a little chewy.

Add 2 cups flour, salt, and baking powder into a large mixing bowl. Stir to mix. Dice the butter into small squares and make sure they're chilled to be very firm. With a sharp knife, cut the cold butter crosswise into 1/4-inch-thick slices. Stack 3 or 4 slices and cut them into three even strips. Rotate the stack a quarter turn and cut the strips in half. You should create 6 small bits of butter per slice. Toss the butter bits into the bowl with the flour mixture. Continue cutting all the butter in the same manner and adding it to the flour mixture. When all the butter is in the bowl with the flour, use your fingers to separate the butter bits (they tend to stick to each other), coat all the butter pieces with flour, and evenly distribute them throughout the flour mixture. What you want is discrete chunks of butter that are clearly visible.

When all the butter is evenly distributed, add the cold buttermilk and stir with a large spoon until all or most of the flour is absorbed by the buttermilk and the dough forms a coarse lump, about 1 minute.

Dust a work surface with flour and dump the dough onto the floured surface, cleaning out the bowl with a spatula or a plastic bowl scraper. Dust the top of the dough and your hands with flour, and press the dough into a 3/4-inch-thick rectangle. Sprinkle a small amount of additional flour on the top of the dough. Fold the dough over on itself in three sections, as if folding a letter (tri-fold). With a bench knife or metal spatula, lift the dough off the counter and dust under it with flour to prevent sticking, if necessary. Dust the top with flour and press the dough out again into a 3/4-inch-thick rectangle and repeat the tri-fold. Repeat this procedure one more time (three times in all). This is similar to how puff pastry is made.

After the three tri-folds dust top with small amount of flour, press out to 1-inch thickness with your fingertips. DO NOT ROLL.

With All Biscuits: Use a cutter with very sharp edges and do not twist when you cut (it compresses the edges). Placing the biscuits close together in the pan will cause them to rise up and will yield tall biscuits with soft sides (classic Southern biscuits).

Leaving a little space between them with make them crusty all over and a bit shorter and wider. It's more common to leave space between flaky biscuits so they're crispy.

Using a larger cutter with either version will give you “cat-head biscuits” (the name simply refers to their size). The traditional way to make a cat-head biscuit is, for each biscuit, to pinch off a piece of dough about the size of a large egg and press it out with your hands. You don't want it to be really flat, it should be relatively biscuit-shaped and about 1 inch high.

If you make tender biscuits out of shortening, and add a little extra buttermilk so they're sloppy, scoop them out as drop biscuits and roll each one in a little flour, and place them close together in the pan, you have the technique behind Shirley Corriher's "Touch of Grace" biscuits.

Bake biscuits in a very hot oven - 500 degrees F/250 degrees C - until they are golden brown, which should take around 15 minutes. In a convection microwave, preheat to 250 degrees C and place on the low rack (low + high if you make 2 trays). Bake for 15 minutes on Convection 200 degrees C. Make sure _NOT_ to grease the pan as it will make the bottoms tough.

Notes and Variations

This is my go-to biscuit dough recipe, I use it for a wide range of things, including desserts and dumplings.

If you don't have buttermilk on hand add 1 Tbsp white vinegar to 3/4 cup milk and let it sit about 10 minutes. I tend to use a large soup spoon full of sour cream or greek yoghurt (I grab a soup spoon out of my silverware drawer). You can leave it in the freezer while it curdles to get really cold.

Australian butter is 1 cup per block. To measure half a cup, cut a block in half diagonally. To measure a quarter cup, split one diagonal in half. American butter is 1/2 cup per stick, so use half a stick.

If your baking powder has an odd metallic taste, you can make your own by mixing together 1 1/2 teaspoons baking soda and 1 1/2 teaspoon cream of tartar to make 3 teaspoons (1 tablespoon) baking powder.

White Lily isn't just a brand name, they use a special grade of soft "winter" wheat for their biscuit flour. A close approximation can be created by blending 1 1/4 cups all purpose flour with 3/4 cup cake flour to make biscuit flour. If you don't have biscuit flour on hand, use all purpose flour but the biscuits will be a little tougher as a result.

You can substitute your preferred solid fat for the butter. 1/4 cup margarine, 1/4 cup leaf lard, or 1/4 cup Crisco can all be used . All will make tender or flaky baked biscuits as preferred. Do a trial run with other solid fats to test. Do not use lard for dumplings as it melts into the water.

To make a sweet dough add 2 Tbsp sugar for a single recipe. This would be good for scones, shortcake, or other desserts.
