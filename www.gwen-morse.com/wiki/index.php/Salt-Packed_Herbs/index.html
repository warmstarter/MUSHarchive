Salt-Packed Herbs

From Gwen Morse's Wiki
Jump to: navigation, search

Since moving to Australia I've been having trouble with purchasing and using fresh herbs. In the US, it was simple. I could go to any local supermarket and pick up very small, affordable packages of fresh herbs that would be used up within a day or two.

In Australia, fresh herbs are both less common and more expensive (even accounting for conversion), and rather curiously sold in very large packages. This means, in order to use a fresh herb, I have to hunt it down and and then cook with it for the next week straight. This gets boring quickly. My solution has been to start chopping and salting some herbs. The salt absorbs some of the excess liquid in the jar and helps to keep the herb from spoiling. It can last for six months to to a year, even without refrigeration.

Salt-Packed Herbs

Finely chop (mince) the fresh herb. A bullet blender or food processor helps.

Measure the herb after it is chopped. For every 4 parts of fresh, finely chopped herb add 1 part kosher, pickling, or sea salt. You want a salt that has somewhat large flakes and no added iodine. Stir until well combined. Pack into a clean glass jar, cover, and refrigerate or store in a pantry.

To use, leave out any other salt called for in the recipe you are making. Add to sauces instead of plain salt. It can also be used as a rub on meat, fish, or poultry, or added to a unsalted butter to make a compound butter.

Note:

  • This 4 to 1 ratio of fresh ingredients to salt can be used to pack herbs that don't dry well, such as cilantro, basil, chives, parsley and rosemary.
  • It can be turned into a simple and delicious dip by adding 3/4 teaspoon of salt-packed herb plus 1/2 teaspoon of minced garlic to 1 1/2 cups of sour cream or greek yogurt.


Herbes Salées

The French tern "Herbes Salées" is best known as the Acadian variation of salt-packed herbs. Acadians are the Canadian ancestors of American Cajuns. Although salt-packed herb mix is eaten around the world, "Herbes Salées" has a particular connotation to the Acadian people.

In it's simplest form, it's a combination of root vegetables and herbs that are finely chopped and mixed with salt. It would have been made throughout the late summer and fall as the the herbs and root vegetables were ripe and being stored.

The mix would have been put in a large crock or covered bowl. The bottom would be covered with a layer of coarse salt (something like kosher salt, sea salt, or pickling salt). Then, as something ripened, it would be chopped up and added to the container, and covered with another layer of salt. As each new crop matured, it would be cut up and added, then the whole thing mixed briskly. When the last of the crops were stored any excess liquid would have been drained away and it would be packed in smaller jars or measured from the storage crock for use throughout the year.

The "traditional" Acadian recipe is hotly debated (as any "traditional" recipe often is). It often includes chervil, parsley, green and white onions, carrots and celery leaves (all in equal proportion). Summer savory is another popular addition.

I remember my Sicilian great-grandmother making something like this that included green and white onion, fennel bulb, basil, oregano, and other "Italian" herbs she grew in her garden.

If you wanted to give it a little more of an American Cajun spin, you could start with the "Holy Trinity" of Cajun cooking: onion, celery, and bell pepper, and include some fresh herbs.

Moving back to Italian culture, you could store a northern Italian soffritto (mixed green and white onions, celery, and carrots) or battuto/odori (celery, carrots, garlic and parsley, with mixed white and green onions). If you wanted a southern Italian or Sicilian base, make sure it has garlic, fennel, and basil along with green herbs like parsley and oregano.

The Polish Włoszczyzna is a descendant of the battuto, and, according to Wikipedia, has carrots, parsnips or parsley root, celery root or celeriac, leeks and savoy or white cabbage leaves, and sometimes celery leaves and flat-leaf parsley. When I want to make the Polish mix I just make sure it has cabbage and parsnips in with some other vegetables. If I feel ambitious enough to clean out all the grit, I'll include some leeks.

Indian cusine has a base of garlic, onion and ginger that translates well to salt packing. Unfortunately, my husband doesn't like ginger so I rarely bother to make it.

When I make the Acadian version, I finely chop a handful of large green onion stalks, the top third to half of a bunch of celery, 3 carrots, 1-2 red onions, and whatever other "greens" (fresh herbs) I have on hand. Last time I made it I added parsley and basil.

To make salt-packed herbs, finely chop each ingredient in a food processor or bullet blender. I turn the whole mess out into my 4-cup Pyrex measuring cup, and add an appropriate amount of salt. I have some recycled jars from pasta sauce on hand, but you can go for something

I'll use the leftover vegetables (that didn't go into the mix) to make a vegetable stock at the same time.

This isn't one of those "exact measurement" preparation techniques, nor is it likely to be measured exactly when added to food. I'll toss a small teaspoon (from the silverware drawer) into the egg mix of an omelette, or dump a larger "serving spoon" sized blob onto new potatoes before baking them, or into mashed potatoes while mixing in the milk. If I add it to a large pot of stock or pasta sauce, I'll add as much as half a cup. It all depends on the amount of food I'm seasoning.

A note about Mushrooms: Poles and Russians like to eat salted/pickled mushrooms. I don't know much about the Russian method (other than it's used to make some nasty mushrooms edible), but the Polish method involved blanching the mushrooms in salted hot water for about 5 minutes and then layering with salt in a crock. Leave the mushrooms whole but separate the caps and the stems. Mix together some fresh dill, juniper berries, salt, pepper, and garlic to form a spice blend. Then, add salt, then a single layer of mushrooms, then a thin spread of spice blend repeatedly until all mushrooms are packed. Cover with a plate or other heavy item (something needs to press the mushrooms down). Add enough water to submerge the mushrooms in brine. Let sit for 5-7 days and then move to smaller individual crocks or jars.

Before serving soak some mushrooms in fresh, _unsalted_ water for a few minutes to help absorb extra salt. Eat with rye bread. This is a snack similar to a pickle.

I've salt-packed diced mushrooms on their own, but never added them to a herbes salées. Always blanch mushrooms before brining or salt-packing or they can turn some pretty disgusting colors.

Herbed Salts

If you instead want to make a herb-flavored salt, reverse the proportions (use 1 part fresh herb to 4 parts salt). If you want to add citrus zest, add 1 teaspoon per cup of salt.
