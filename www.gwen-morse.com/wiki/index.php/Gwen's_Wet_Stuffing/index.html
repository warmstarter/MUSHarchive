Gwen's Wet Poultry Stuffing

  • one small package fresh mushrooms (or half a large package), sliced
  • 3 stalks celery, chopped
  • a mix of chopped walnuts and whole pine nuts equalling 1 cup total
  • chopped and cooked poultry giblets
  • 1/2 pound sausage meat
  • 1/4 cup melted salted butter
  • a couple cloves of garlic, mashed and chopped
  • 1 small onion, diced (or half a red onion)
  • 1 egg
  • 1/2 a loaf of bread
  • ~ 1/4 cup poultry stock
  • 1 bunch fresh parsley
  • a drizzle of walnut oil

This should be enough for a roasting chicken. For serving with a turkey, double all ingredients.

Directions: melt the butter in a large skillet or saucepan over medium heat, add in the first 8 ingredients (except the bread, and stopping after the onion) one at a time, browning the contents in between. Make sure the bits of giblets and the sausage meat are THOROUGHLY cooked. Remove from the heat. Add in any liquid from the mushrooms (if canned). Salt and pepper to taste. Add finely minced fresh herbs if you have them, and some Italian seasoning mix. The chopped leafy tops of the celery make a slightly bitter but tasty addition.

For the nuts, I use about 1/4 cup of pine nuts, and enough whole walnuts to fill to the line. Then I chop the walnuts after I measure.

When everything is cooked thoroughly, add the egg, the stuffing cubes, and the walnut oil to the final baking pan or crock pot. Add enough stock (about 1/4 cup) to moisten the stuffing cubes.

In Australia, I use chorizo sausage. There's nothing like the "sweet" Sicilian fennel sausage I'm used to getting in the U.S. If I can find a butcher to make some, I want: fennel based salsiccia fresca.

Because it's potentially unsafe to cook stuffing inside a bird, there's two options for cooking this stuffing:

  • To bake, transfer to a 9x13 inch baking dish or other large casserole dish. Bake uncovered for 45 minutes to 1 hour at 350 degrees F (175 degrees C) or for the last hour while the turkey is roasting. This version of the stuffing ends up dry and crunchy.
  • It can also be cooked in a crock pot. Cook on High for 45 minutes, then reduce heat to Low, and cook for 4 to 8 hours. It's very important to thoroughly stir the stuffing once per hour, or it may stick to the sides of the crock pot and burn. It will go through various wet and dry cycles and it's a good idea to cook it with the lid off for the last two hours to keep it from being soggy. If you add the recommended 1/4 cup of chicken or turkey stock to the crock pot, it will end up with that nice poultry taste it gets from being roasted in the bird. It's not exactly as good as roasting in the turkey, but it's pretty close and much more hygienic.

Variations:

  • Low-carb - replace stuffing cubes with extra mushrooms and walnuts.
  • Gluten free - make croutons out of gluten-free bread.
  • Cornbread stuffing - replace the bread cubes with a half batch of Ben's Favorite Cornbread. Or a full batch for a turkey.
  • Italian-American - coat the bread in olive oil before toasting it, add some Genoa salami (cut into slivers like matchsticks), cut a block or ball of mozzarella into small cubes. Mix all into the stuffing.

Home Made Bread Croutons: Any bread will work just let it dry out a bit first.

  • Placing slices on a cookie sheet for 30 minutes at 250 degrees F (175 degrees C) will work.
  • Placing pre-cubed bread pieces on a cookie sheet for 15 minutes at 100 degrees F (200 degrees C) will also work.

The idea is to toast the bread dry enough so the bread will be slightly crispy and not squish together in a giant dough ball when you mix the stuffing. When making this recipe, one loaf of bread is enough for a full turkey so use a 1/2 loaf or even just a few slices for a chicken. If you bake your own bread to make the croutons, you can bake in fresh herbs (1 loaf bread: 1 tablespoon sage, 1 teaspoon thyme, 1/8 teaspoon fresh black pepper).

Further notes: The stuffing is "wet" because when used as an actual stuffing it absorbs liquid inside the bird as it bakes to make it moist and fluffy. As mentioned above I now simulate this by cooking it in a crockpot.

If you can't get giblets, you can try grinding up some chicken hearts in your food processor.

Metric Sizes and Turkey Cooking Times

  • 6 Kilos = 13 pounds
  • 8.5 kilos = 18.7 pounds = 300 minutes = 5 hours
  • 9 kilos = 20 pounds

I prefer a 6 kilo turkey for Thanksgiving and a 9 kilo turkey for Christmas. This way we don't have so many leftovers after Thanksgiving that we're sick of turkey when Christmas comes, and then for Christmas we have a lot of leftovers.

Thawing the Turkey:

According to the USDA, here are guidelines for thawing a whole turkey in the refrigerator:

  • 4 to 12 pounds: one to three days
  • 12 to 16 pounds: three to four days
  • 16 to 20 pounds: four to five days
  • 20 to 24 pounds: five to six days

For thawing in a fridge at 4°C, UKMA recommends allowing 12 hours per kilogram. Thus to thaw an 8 kg turkey allow 4 days.

From the BBC web site

Preheat the oven to 180C/350F/Gas 4.

  • For a turkey that weighs less than 4.5kg/ 9lb 15oz: roast for 45 mins/kg, or 18 mins/lb plus 20 minutes.
  • For a turkey that weighs between 4.5kg/9lb 15oz and 6.5kg/14lb 5oz: roast for 40 mins/kg, or 16 mins/lb.
  • For a turkey that weighs more than 6.5kg/14lb 5oz: roast for 35 mins/kg, or 14 mins/lb.

Set aside and rest, covered, for 30 minutes before serving.

Specific Turkey Sizes

  • 4.5 kilos = 200 minutes (3 hours and 20 minutes)
