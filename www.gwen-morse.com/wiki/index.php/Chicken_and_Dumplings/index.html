Gwen's Fluffy Yankee-style Chicken and Dumplings

My original source is the White Lily flour bag and cookbook, with a few adaptations.

" White Lily Flour" Dumplings

  • 2 cups biscuit flour or all purpose flour (may adjust for texture, use no more than 3 cups)
  • Pinch of salt
  • 1/4 cup butter, Crisco vegetable shortening, or margarine
  • 3/4 cup Buttermilk (more or less to achieve a sticky dough)

Pour 2 cups flour and add salt and baking powder into a large mixing bowl. Stir to mix. Dice the butter into small squares and make sure they're chilled to be very firm. Add butter and cut into the flour until you get dime-sized chunks of the flour/butter mixture. Mix in buttermilk a little at a time until you get a sticky dough texture.Turn dough onto a floured surface. Dust top with small amount of flour. For fluffy dumplings, roll out to 1/2-inch thickness and cut into 1" x 1" squares or diamonds. For dense dumplings, pull and roll firmly into golf-ball sized balls. I never really enjoyed the super-dense dumplings "true" Southerners love, I prefer mine lighter and fluffier, which is supposedly a Yankee/Northerner preference.

My perfect dumplings are soft all the way through, and the outermost shell is dissolving into the water.

If you don't have buttermilk on hand add 1 Tbsp white vinegar to 3/4 cup milk and let it sit about 10 minutes.

For the chicken:

Put 2-3 packages of market packed chicken drumsticks and/or thighs (or one giant family-sized pack) into a stock pot and add enough water to cover the chicken. Add 2 ribs of celery, a couple cloves of fresh minced garlic, a generous grind of fresh ground pepper (~10-15 grinds) and 1 teaspoon salt. Simmer covered for 30 minutes, then add the dumplings to the pot without removing the chicken, and cook covered for an additional 30 minutes. Letting the dumplings cook while resting on the chicken parts helps keep them fluffy as does leaving the lid on. The dumplings will release starch that thickens the chicken broth. There is no need to de-bone and shred the chicken if you only use dark meat parts, the bones are easy to avoid if eating the meat with a fork.

If you want to de-bone the meat, after the first 30 minute cook time, cool, remove chicken, strain broth, chill and remove fat from the surface. Remove skin and bones; cut or shred the chicken into large bite-size pieces. Return chicken to the pot. Drop in the dumplings, bring the liquid back up to a simmer, and then simmer for an additional 30 minutes.

Notes from Gwen:

Chicken and dumplings in its original form is something of a poverty meal - a way to stretch out a tough old chicken to feed a large family. I prefer to dress it up with a little garlic and celery to give it a bit of a "chicken soup" flair without overwhelming the simple flavor. In it's most basic form it's a cut up whole chicken, enough water to cover, one batch of biscuit dough (with a little salt and pepper) turned into dumplings, and nothing more. You can get as fancy as you like with the stock, but the more you add the further you get away from the original dish.
